Record-Breaking Hot and Cold Days Across the USA

Or, “How I learned to stop worrying and love the data.”

by William Herkewitz

Ever hear someone bewail global warming on a hot day? What about on a cold one?

You’d be hard pressed to name a science that’s more by troubled by people’s fickle experiences than climate science. (If you can name one, please say it out loud in class and shame me during my presentation) The average person’s belief in global warming has far more to do with their political beliefs and subjective experiences than how well they’ve been keeping up with the scientific literature

For my data project, I thought I’d map the record-breaking high and low temperatures across the states. I had hoped to create a tool that, while not showing any scientific relation to global warming (which is far more complicated than, “We’ve had a lot of hot days, the earth must be warming!”), might map out an area where public belief in global warming would likely to be either doubtful or certain.

Below are two maps that chart the number of record hot and cold days (from the approximately 11,000 nationwide weather stations) since 2000 across the USA.

Based on this map, it looks a lot like most of the record hot days have been spread across the Southwest and Northwest. Parts of northern New England also look a little burnt.

The Residents of Kentucky meanwhile–though notorious for their staunch support of science-based evidence–might have more reason to doubt claims of global warming.

Two things here are immediately noticeable.

First, there’s been a lot fewer record-breaking cold days than hot ones. Again, this doesn’t say anything definite about global warming, per se (although it does deal a pretty hard blow to the radical climate-science splinter group: the ‘global-cooling’ proponents).

Second, the record cold temperature days seems spread across roughly the same areas as the record hot days. Again we see a lot of records over the Southwest, Northwest, and northern New England. And the south and Midwest seem relatively devoid of new cold temperature records. Does this mean all the temperature (hot and cold) variation is happening in the same places

Or is something fishy going on?
